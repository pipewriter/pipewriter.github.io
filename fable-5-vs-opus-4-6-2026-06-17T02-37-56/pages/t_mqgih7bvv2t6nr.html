How this works — “Genome Drift”▸ show
Every point in the world carries an 8-gene DNA vector sampled from a vector-valued fractional Brownian field. Because the field is fractal, the genetic distance between two places grows as a power law of their physical distance — so neighbors share almost-identical DNA (similar, never identical), and faraway places drift into wholly foreign genomes. There is no biome grid and no characteristic scale.

A fixed decoder turns each DNA vector into everything you see — base elevation, mountain amplitude, ridge sharpness, terracing, temperature, moisture, the color signature, and the plants. Smooth blending of the DNA between peaks is the “sun-damage” gradient you cross at biome edges.

Each sample is stateless, deterministic, and O(1) in world size: the same coordinate always yields the same land, computed from coordinates alone — so the world is effectively infinite. Press T to leap thousands of meters and watch the DNA go foreign; take a few steps to see it barely shift.
